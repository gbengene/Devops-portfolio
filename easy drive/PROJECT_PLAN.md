# Easy Drive — Project Plan
**A Division of GB Trade and Logistics Inc.**
**Version 1.0 | May 2026**

---

## Overview

**Project:** Launch Easy Drive — a peer-to-peer car rental marketplace for GTA gig drivers
**Legal entity:** GB Trade and Logistics Inc. (existing)
**Day 0:** Project start (Week 1, May 2026)
**Launch target:** Platform live with 10 approved host listings and 20 approved renters by Week 8
**Owner:** Founder / Admin (GB Trade and Logistics Inc.)

---

## 1. Work Streams

Five parallel work streams run simultaneously from Day 0. They are interdependent — the critical path section identifies where delays cascade.

### WS-1: Legal & Compliance
Register the trade name, secure documents, and set up the entity's commercial and payment infrastructure. Nothing ships to users until the ToS, Host Agreement, and Renter Agreement are signed off by a lawyer.

### WS-2: Platform Development (Tech)
Build and deploy the minimum viable platform: host portal (listing application, availability, earnings), renter portal (application, browse, book, pay), and admin portal (approval queues, GPS view, kill switch controls). Stripe Connect, Supabase, Bouncie integration, PandaDoc for rental agreements, Twilio for SMS.

### WS-3: Host Acquisition (Supply)
Recruit, onboard, and approve the first 10 host listings before launch day. White-glove onboarding for first hosts — visit in person, verify insurance, assist with Bouncie installation. Supply must precede demand.

### WS-4: Renter Acquisition (Demand)
Build a waitlist of approved renters before launch. Post in Facebook groups, WhatsApp gig communities, and distribute physical flyers. Activate referral program on launch day.

### WS-5: Operations Setup
Define all operational playbooks: booking workflow, payment failure protocol, kill switch protocol, dispute resolution, vehicle inspection checklist, weekly payout process. These must exist in writing before the first live booking.

## 3. Critical Path

These are the 7 tasks that, if delayed, delay everything else. Each one is a blocking dependency for downstream work.

### CP-1: Trade Name Registration (Week 1)
**Why it's critical:** Every public-facing action — signing host agreements, collecting payments, running ads, publishing the platform — requires "Easy Drive" to be a legally registered name. Without it, the entity cannot operate commercially. This is a same-day Ontario Business Registry action. No excuse for delay.

### CP-2: Lawyer Retained and Documents Finalized (Weeks 2–3)
**Why it's critical:** No host can be formally onboarded without a signed Host Agreement. No renter can be accepted without a signed Renter ToS. No booking can be executed without a Rental Agreement. If legal documents are delayed past Week 3, the entire Week 5–8 schedule compresses and the launch date slips. Engage the lawyer in Week 1 conversations; formally retain by Week 2 start.

### CP-3: Stripe Connect Business Verification (Week 2)
**Why it's critical:** Stripe's business verification process (KYC for the platform account) can take 2–5 business days and may require additional document submission. If this is not started in Week 2, the payment flow cannot be tested in Week 5 and the booking flow cannot go live in Week 7. This is the most common source of unexpected payment launch delays.

### CP-4: Host Insurance Verification Process (Weeks 3–5)
**Why it's critical:** Every host application is gated on the founder manually verifying that their insurance certificate explicitly covers commercial delivery use. This cannot be delegated to an automated system at launch. If the founder does not learn to read Ontario insurance certificates and identify the commercial-use endorsement (Intact OPCF 6A, Aviva equivalent), invalid listings will go live — creating uninsured vehicles on the platform and catastrophic liability exposure. This check must be done personally for every one of the first 10 hosts.

### CP-5: First 10 Host Listings Approved (by Week 8 launch date)
**Why it's critical:** The platform cannot launch publicly without supply. Renters will not apply and the platform has no value without vehicles to rent. The entire demand-side acquisition effort is predicated on supply existing first. 10 is the minimum viable supply for a credible launch. Host acquisition must begin in Week 3 so there is time to find, visit, and approve hosts before Week 8. Starting host outreach later than Week 3 creates serious risk of not hitting this number.

### CP-6: Booking Flow End-to-End Tested in Staging (Week 5–6)
**Why it's critical:** The booking flow touches Stripe (payment collection), Supabase (booking record), PandaDoc (rental agreement generation), Twilio (SMS confirmation), and Resend (email). A failure in any of these integrations means money is collected without a contract, or a contract is signed without GPS being active, or a payout fires incorrectly. This cannot be tested for the first time with real money. A full end-to-end test in staging — with a test renter, test host, and test card — must be complete before Week 7 soft launch.

### CP-7: Operational Playbooks Written Before First Live Booking (Week 6)
**Why it's critical:** The payment failure protocol, kill switch activation protocol, and dispute resolution process must exist in writing before the first renter hands over $500. Without these playbooks, the founder will improvise under pressure — and improvised kill switch activations or deposit decisions create legal exposure. These documents take 2–3 hours to write and protect the business from the most predictable incidents.

## 8. Weekly KPIs

The founder reviews these every Monday morning. Each metric has a target range. Any metric outside the target range triggers a written note: what happened, why, and what action is being taken.

| # | Metric | How to Measure | Healthy Range | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| KPI-1 | Active Listings (approved, currently rented) | Admin portal — count of bookings with status "active" | Month 1: 3–5; Month 2: 8–10; Month 3+: growing week-over-week | Flat or declining for 2+ consecutive weeks |
| KPI-2 | Listing Utilization Rate | Active rentals / Total approved listings | 80%+ (industry minimum for viability) | Below 70% — vehicles are idle; pricing or demand issue |
| KPI-3 | New Host Applications This Week | Admin portal — host application queue | Month 1–2: 2–5/week; Month 3+: 3–8/week | Zero applications for 2 consecutive weeks — outreach has stopped working |
| KPI-4 | New Renter Applications This Week | Admin portal — renter application queue | Month 1–2: 5–10/week; Month 3+: 10–20/week | Zero applications for 2 consecutive weeks — demand channel is broken |
| KPI-5 | Platform Fee Collected (weekly) | Stripe dashboard — platform's 20% retained | Week 8: $350–$700; Month 3: $700–$1,200; Month 6: $2,000+ | Below break-even ($235/month per car) for any active listing |
| KPI-6 | Host Payout Accuracy | Count of payout errors / total payouts processed | Zero errors per week | Any payout error — investigate immediately; host trust is fragile early |
| KPI-7 | Payment Failures (renters) | Stripe dashboard — failed charges | 0–1 per week at launch; threshold rises with volume | More than 1 unresolved failure at any time — kill switch standby engaged |
| KPI-8 | Time to Approve Host Application | Days from submission to approved or rejected with feedback | Under 48 hours | Over 72 hours — hosts lose interest if kept waiting |
| KPI-9 | Time to Approve Renter Application | Days from submission to approved or rejected | Under 48 hours | Over 72 hours — renters book elsewhere |
| KPI-10 | Active Renter Retention Rate | Renters who renewed for a second week / total who completed first week | 75%+ (first 30 days); 85%+ (Month 3+) | Below 60% — something is wrong with the vehicle, the host, or the platform experience |

**Weekly KPI Review Protocol:**

Every Monday, the founder opens the admin portal and Stripe dashboard and records these 10 numbers in a simple tracking spreadsheet. Any metric outside the healthy range gets a 2-sentence note. This takes 20 minutes and is the single most important operational habit in the first 6 months.
